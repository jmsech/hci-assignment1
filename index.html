Blackboard Redesign

A virtual "home base" for learning .

Blackboard is a virtual learning platform that is imagined as a “home base” for many high school and university classes. Unfortunately, its cluttered and inefficient design forces many teachers into using additional 3rd party platforms in order to host all of the necessary content for their course.

Students and teachers alike deserve a single, streamlined platform that will minimize the time spent navigate their virtual classroom and maximize the time (and ability) to learn effctively. Blackboard has many great features, and with a user interface redesign, I believe the web service can realize its much fuller potential as an all-in-one virtual learning service.

Design Focus and Problem Space

Primary Users

Blackboard is designed as a way for teachers to distribute any and all course material, assigents, and announcements to students online. In this redesign, I will be focusing on Blackboard’s Student View.

Key Problems

  • Inefficient use of screen space (many surface-level features go untouched)
  • Many key features are hidden from direct access and take many clicks to reach
  • Announcements and other key information or assignments can easily become buried in the confusing and cluttered layout

Deisgn Focus

While Blackboard has an array of separate pages, tabs, and functions, I will focus my redesign on the Courses tab as this most directly impacts the Target Users of this redesign (students).

Contextual Inquiry

Steven Solar, a Junior Computer Science and Biomedical Engineering student at Johns Hopkins University, served as the test subject for contextual inquiry. Steven has used Blackboard for many of his courses at Hopkins and is a good subject to observe navigating the webservice. We are a few months into the semester and students have had time to familiarize themselves with the layout of each class on Blackboard. I wanted to capture the experience of navigating Blackboard at the start of a semester when things are still relatively unfamiliar. Instead of having Steven navigate his own courses, I asked Steven to use my Blackboard account to perform each task. This way he is familiar with the general mechanics and layout of Blackboard, but was now acting as if he had just stared the semester with a new set of courses.

Task 1: Find A Course Syllabus

The subject was asked to find the syllabus for his Computer Ethics class. He had no trouble navigating to the class’ home page, but he was expecting to find the syllabus linked directly on the home page or in the main toolbar on the left hand side of the page. When he didn’t find it in either of those two places he clicked into “Course Content” from the main LHS toolbar. From there, he was able to continue clicking through folders until he pulled up the syllabus.

    Key Takeway:
  • The subject thought the syllabus was important enough to be accessible from the courses home screen

Task 2: Check Grades

The subject was asked to view his grades for the current course, Computer Ethics. He had no trouble instantly clicking the “My Grades” link from the main toolbar. However, when asked to navigate to his grades for other classes he was completely lost. He couldn’t find any quick link to view all courses and ended up having to exit to the separate top-right-corner dropdown menu. He was unfamiliar with what the various icons meant and eventually clicked on the correct one. The subject was still confused where his other courses’ grades were (even though they were on the current screen), but he eventually realized he had been interpreting the display incorrectly and noted that nothing about what he had just done felt intuitive. Additionally, he voiced that he wished the "All Grades" menu listed the course name first rather than the number grade.

    Key Takeways:
  • It is nice (and easy) to have each courses' grades linked directly from the home screen
  • It needs to be more obvious which couse you are currently viewing in the "All Courses" view of grades adn you should be able to easily click between different classes
  • The icons in the top RHS dropdown menu are ambiguous and their functions are unclear

Task 3: Submit An Assignment

The subject was asked to find and submit the current assignment for Computer Ethics. He quickly clicked on “Student Assesment” from the main toolbar but immediately second guessed himself and clicked on “Course Content”. After realizing the assignment wasn’t under “Course Content”, he returned to the “Student Assesment” page and scrolled to the bottom. He (incorrectly) clicked the last assignment on the page and navigated to the submit button.

    Key Takeways:
  • Some courses place assignments in “Course Content” and others place them in “Student Assesment”. This is detrimental to work flow and only serves to cause confusion.
  • The most current assignment should be displayed on the main screen, while any past or future assignments should be hidden to reduce clutter and confusion.

Task 4: Read Announcments

The subject was asked to read any announcements on his account. His first response was to ask to himself “where’s the announcements tab?” (such tab doesnt exist on the toolbar). He clicked the “Home Page” link on the toolbar, however he was already in the home page menu. This confused the subject as to why the “Home Page” button appeared when he was already home. He clicked to “Course Content” only to not find what he was looking for and then returned to the home page. He finally scrolled down in the home page, found the “Discussion Board” at the bottom, and opened the unread message.

    Key Takeways:
  • Announcements are buried in the current layout and therefor go overlooked (the subject admitted to not using the Announcements function for his courses).
  • A course’s most recent (unread) announcement should be displayed or previewed on the home page of each course.
